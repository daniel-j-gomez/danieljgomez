About Me

Disciplines: Cancer Neuroscience | Translational Medical Science | Structural Biology, Biophysics, and Biochemistry | Microbiology & Immunology

Current Mission: Learn advanced imaging techniques that study the structure of individual biological macromolecules at atomic resolution. Become adept at protein expression and purification for human relevant studies. This includes many protein biochemistry and protein (enzyme) engineering techniques that pertain to structural biology. Also, I'm learning about nanobiotechnology tools that can facilitate bio-conjugation, make post-translational modifications, and soon I will produce and screen nanobodies for research tools (e.g., enable Cryo-EM), diagnostics, therapeutics, imaging, targeted drug discovery, and bio-sensing and detection. I am constantly finding patterns and learning advanced mathematics in structural molecular/cellular biology sophisticated algorithms that can reconstruct a three-dimensional image of the sample's (ultra)structure. My goal is to study my heterogeneous samples in different conformational states and/or orientations to provide valuable insights into the structure dynamics and function (shape and motion) of the supramolecular complex of biomolecules.

Research Interests: My research interests lie in the application of cutting-edge techniques in structural biology and bioengineering to develop new and more effective therapies for diseases. Specifically, I am interested in studying the dynamics of supramolecular structures in response to ligand binding and enzymatic reactions using time-resolved structural biology techniques like cryogenic electron microscopy and tomography. I also aim to explore novel approaches like computational biology and bioinformatics for macromolecular engineering and drug design. Additionally, I am fascinated by the study of organelles and their roles in cellular function and disease, and I am learning cutting-edge imaging techniques: cryogenic electron microscopy and tomography for high-resolution bioimaging of organelles in live cells to enable new diagnostic and therapeutic approaches for diseases: cancer, neurodegenerative disorders, and infectious diseases.

Research

Project III: Structural Biology of Supramolecular Complexes in Nutrient-Sensing
MS University Thesis This research project focuses on investigating cellular processes related to lysosomes and other unidentified organelles in the context of cancer. Specifically, it explores the mTORC1 network, which is known to play a crucial role in various types of cancer driven by oncogenes. The objective is to obtain CryoEM structures of unidentified protein complexes within this network to unravel their structural details and functional implications. The project aims to analyze supramolecular complexes, a term used to describe the intricate assemblies of proteins and other molecules involved in cellular processes. By employing CryoEM, the project seeks to provide structural elucidation of these unidentified protein complexes. The ultimate goal is to gain a deeper understanding of their roles in cancer development and progression, ultimately contributing to the validation of high-resolution macromolecular structures of therapeutic value. Furthermore, this research project aims to contribute to the advancement and revolutionization of structural biology. It entails pushing the boundaries of CryoEM techniques by overcoming challenges such as increasing the molecular weight limit (kDa), enhancing resolution, addressing sample heterogeneity, and conducting dynamic and time-resolved studies. These innovations in CryoEM will pave the way for more comprehensive and detailed investigations into cellular processes, opening new avenues for understanding cancer biology and potentially identifying novel therapeutic targets.

Project II: Exploring Host-Pathogen Interactions and Organelle Dysfunction in Disease Research
Theme: This research project focuses on investigating the mechanisms of host-pathogen interactions and organelle dysfunction in the context of disease development. The project involves both experimental and computational methods to unravel the molecular and cellular basis of disease pathogenesis.
Summary: The research project encompasses several posters and publications that highlight different aspects of host-pathogen interactions and organelle dysfunction in disease research. The posters presented at the Microbiology Student Group Symposium and the AACR-JCA Joint Conference focus on co-infection and cancer, specifically the role of viral oncogenesis in liver, blood, and brain cancer by host-pathogen interactions. The publications delve into the structural dynamics of human Pegivirus-1 RNA-dependent RNA polymerase, epitope-based vaccines and immunotherapy for infection-related cancers in people living with HIV, and the dysfunctional Golgi apparatus in disease research. The project aims to advance our understanding of the mechanisms underlying disease development and to identify potential targets for therapeutic intervention.

Project I: Undergraduate Research Opportunities Program (UROP)
Title: Interleukin-17 Production in CNS by Infiltrating T Cells and Glial Cells in the HIV-1-Infected Brain
Summary: Despite effective combined anti-retroviral therapy (cART) for HIV-1-infected individuals, neurocognitive complications persist in patients, leading to negative impacts on daily functioning. The central nervous system (CNS) is affected in multiple ways during HIV infection, as HIV-infected cells cross the blood-brain-barrier (BBB) and initiate events causing CNS damage and neurocognitive problems. This study aims to investigate the role of interleukin-17 (IL-17) in HIV-associated neurocognitive disorders (HAND), as IL-17 can disrupt the BBB. The researchers will analyze brain specimens and cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) samples from HAND patients with HIV encephalitis (HIVE) and compare them to samples from HIV-infected individuals with normal cognition (NC). The hypothesis is that IL-17A will be found in subcortical structures, specifically in the medium-spiny neurons of the basal ganglia and interneurons in the hippocampus, and that astrocytic secretion of the TH17 cell chemokine (CCL20) and cytokine (IL-17) will be detected in CSF samples of HAND patients with HIVE.
